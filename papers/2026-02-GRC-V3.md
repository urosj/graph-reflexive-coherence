## Abstract

We present **Graph Reflexive Coherence V3 (GRC‑v3)** as the next discrete realization of Reflexive Coherence. The extension is motivated by a precise limitation of GRC‑v2: a vertex stores only a scalar coherence value $C_i$, so a non-uniform identity basin can be represented only by tiling it with many connected nodes. That approximation is operationally useful but geometrically indirect, because the internal gradient, curvature, flux orientation, and effective basin size are not stored where the theory says identity actually lives. GRC‑v3 resolves this by equipping each vertex with a **basin-attribute bundle** that stores a local differential summary of the basin it represents: coherence density, discrete gradient, discrete Hessian, net flux summary, effective basin size, and hierarchy labels. In parallel, GRC‑v3 separates the single v2 conductance into a **dynamical conductance** used by the update equations and a family of **analytic edge labels** used for interpretation: geometric length, temporal propagation delay, and flux coupling strength. This preserves the closed reflexive loop of GRC‑v2 while restoring a closer correspondence with the continuous RC theory and the distance constructions of the RC geometry paper.

## Roadmap (what changes and what does not)

GRC‑v3 should be read as a refinement of GRC‑v2, not a replacement of its reflexive logic.

1. **What stays fixed.** The closed loop remains
   $$
   C \rightarrow K[C] \rightarrow w \rightarrow \Phi \rightarrow J \rightarrow C,
   $$
   together with continuity, budget conservation, identity basins, spark-driven topology change, front propagation, and abundance.

2. **What changes at the node level.** A node is no longer interpreted as a uniform scalar cell only. It becomes a **basin chart**: a compact local summary of a non-uniform identity basin.

3. **What changes at the edge level.** The conductance used by the dynamics remains explicit, but distance-like and coupling-like questions are no longer forced into that one number. We therefore store additional edge labels derived from the same state.

4. **What this buys us.** Spark detection can use direct curvature data instead of only proxies, hierarchy becomes explicit, and the graph can answer three different questions without conflating them:
   geometric separation, temporal reachability, and functional coupling.

## Core objects – the enriched skeleton

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| $\mathcal G^{(k)}=(V^{(k)},E^{(k)})$ | Weighted graph at discrete time-step $k$. |
| $C_i^{(k)}\ge 0$ | Coherence density stored at node $i$. |
| $\mathcal B_i^{(k)}$ | **Basin-attribute bundle** attached to node $i$. |
| $w_{ij}^{(k)}>0$ | **Base conductance** on edge $(i,j)$; this is the weight used by the actual dynamics. |
| $\ell_{ij}^{(d)},\ \tau_{ij},\ F_{ij}$ | Geometric length, temporal delay, and flux-coupling labels derived from the same state. |
| $J_{ij}^{(k)}=-J_{ji}^{(k)}$ | Directed coherence flux on the edge. |
| $\Phi_i^{(k)}$ | Coherence potential (discrete variational derivative of the graph functional). |

The basin-attribute bundle is

<span style="float:right;">(1)</span>

$$
\mathcal B_i^{(k)}=
\Big(
C_i,\ \mathbf g_i,\ H_i,\ \mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net}},\ M_i,\ \mathrm{id}_i,\ \mathrm{parent}_i,\ \mathrm{depth}_i
\Big)^{(k)},
$$

where:

- $\mathbf g_i$ is a discrete gradient summary,
- $H_i$ is a discrete Hessian summary,
- $\mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net}}$ is the net flux vector summary,
- $M_i$ is an effective basin size or basin mass indicator,
- and the last three entries encode hierarchy.

The important point is semantic: **the dynamics is still local, but the node now stores the local differential information that GRC‑v2 had to infer indirectly from topology.**

**Axiomatic vs constitutive components.** As in GRC‑v2, the axiomatic commitments are:

- coherence as a conserved scalar budget,
- geometry generated from coherence,
- flux as constitutive response to coherence potential,
- identity as an emergent attractor structure,
- and reflexive operator closure under topology change.

The particular formulas below are a canonical realization of that class.

## Why GRC‑v2 is not enough for non-uniform basins

In the continuous RC theory, an identity basin is not defined merely by a label or a high scalar value. It is a geometric region whose identity depends on internal structure: a center or core, inward-pointing gradients, a stable curvature signature, and a coherent flux organization. The distance paper makes the same point from the geometric side: once geometry is induced from coherence, spatial separation, travel time, and dynamic coupling need not collapse to one number.

GRC‑v2 is faithful at the level of the reflexive loop, but it compresses each node to one scalar $C_i$. If a basin is non-uniform, there are only two options:

- treat the basin as uniform and lose the internal geometric information, or
- approximate the basin by many graph nodes and let edges mimic the internal variation.

The second option is often useful computationally, but it has three costs.

1. **Artificial internal boundaries.** What should be one basin becomes many vertices separated by explicit graph edges.
2. **Indirect spark detection.** The continuous spark condition is curvature degeneracy; in v2 this is usually detected through a topological or spectral proxy.
3. **Conflated edge meaning.** The same weight is asked to behave as conductance, distance, delay, and coupling strength.

GRC‑v3 addresses exactly these three gaps while keeping the same closed loop.

## Basin-attribute nodes – discrete differential summaries

The key move of GRC‑v3 is to store at each node the differential summaries that matter for basin identity.

### Discrete gradient

Let $\mathcal N(i)$ be the neighbours of $i$, and let $\mathbf e_{ij}$ denote a bookkeeping direction from $i$ to $j$ in the local edge basis. We define the weighted gradient summary

<span style="float:right;">(2)</span>

$$
\mathbf g_i^{(k)}=
\frac{1}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}\big(C_j^{(k)}-C_i^{(k)}\big)\mathbf e_{ij}.
$$

This is the same local information that v2 already uses implicitly in its flux and Laplacian computations, but here it is stored as an explicit state summary.

### Discrete Hessian

The second-order summary is

<span style="float:right;">(3)</span>

$$
H_i^{(k)}=
\frac{1}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}\big(C_j^{(k)}-C_i^{(k)}\big)\,
\mathbf e_{ij}\otimes \mathbf e_{ij}.
$$

The eigenstructure of $H_i$ plays the role that the Hessian plays in the continuous theory:

- large-magnitude stable eigenvalues indicate a well-formed basin geometry,
- a small eigenvalue indicates flattening in one direction,
- and near-degeneracy signals a spark candidate.

To avoid sign-convention ambiguity across different potential conventions, GRC‑v3 uses the **spectrum relative to the chosen basin convention** and treats spark formation as a loss of non-degeneracy rather than hard-coding one sign.

### Net flux summary

The node-wise net flux summary is

<span style="float:right;">(4)</span>

$$
\mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net},(k)}=\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} J_{ij}^{(k)}\,\mathbf e_{ij}.
$$

This is not a new primitive; it is a compact record of the edge fluxes already generated by the reflexive loop.

### Basin size and hierarchy

For the discrete basin represented by node $i$, GRC‑v3 stores an effective size or mass indicator

<span style="float:right;">(5)</span>

$$
M_i^{(k)} := \sum_{u\in \mathcal A_i^{(k)}} C_u^{(k)},
$$

where $\mathcal A_i^{(k)}$ is the current attraction set associated to the identity represented by $i$. In practice this can be computed exactly from a directed-flux flood fill, or updated incrementally after local events.

Hierarchy is encoded by:

- `id`: the current basin identity,
- `parent`: the parent basin if the node arose by refinement of a larger basin,
- `depth`: the nesting level.

This lets GRC‑v3 represent repeated spark-driven refinement as an explicit tree or DAG of identities rather than as a flat collection of sinks.

## Geometry from basin attributes – tensor, conductance, and analytic distances

GRC‑v3 keeps the same geometric logic as GRC‑v2: coherence generates geometry, geometry shapes flux, and flux updates coherence. The difference is that the gradient and flux summaries used in geometry are now stored explicitly on the nodes.

### Node tensor

The canonical node tensor becomes

<span style="float:right;">(6)</span>

$$
K_i^{(k)}
=
\lambda_C C_i^{(k)} I
+ \xi_C\, \mathbf g_i^{(k)}\otimes \mathbf g_i^{(k)}
+ \zeta_C\, \mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net},(k)}\otimes \mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net},(k)}.
$$

This is the discrete analogue of the RC coherence tensor expressed in the local graph basis. It differs from the v2 presentation mainly in where the differential information lives: in v2 it had to be reconstructed on the fly; in v3 it is already part of the node state.

### Base conductance for the actual dynamics

The weight that drives the dynamical equations remains a strictly positive conductance:

<span style="float:right;">(7)</span>

$$
w_{ij}^{(k+1)}
=
\exp\!\left[
-\alpha \frac{C_i+C_j}{2}
-\beta \frac{\| \mathbf g_i-\mathbf g_j\|^2}{2}
-\gamma \frac{J_{ij}^2}{2}
-\delta\,\kappa^{\mathrm{Ric}}_{ij}
\right].
$$

This keeps the role of $w_{ij}$ clean: **it is the conductance used by the dynamics.**

### Three analytic edge labels

The distance paper showed that RC naturally distinguishes at least three notions that need not coincide: geometric distance, operational travel time, and flux-supported relation. GRC‑v3 therefore stores three additional edge labels.

1. **Geometric length.** Using a symmetric edge tensor $\bar K_{ij}=\tfrac12(K_i+K_j)$ and an optional embedding displacement $\Delta x_{ij}$ when such a chart exists, define

<span style="float:right;">(8)</span>

$$
\ell_{ij}^{(d)}
=
\sqrt{\Delta x_{ij}^{\!\top}\,\bar K_{ij}\,\Delta x_{ij}}.
$$

When no ambient embedding is used, $\ell_{ij}^{(d)}$ can be replaced by any equivalent graph-intrinsic monotone derived from $w_{ij}$ and the local tensors.

2. **Temporal delay.** Distinguish shortest from fastest by defining

<span style="float:right;">(9)</span>

$$
\tau_{ij}
=
\frac{\ell_{ij}^{(d)}}{v_0+\rho |J_{ij}|+\varepsilon_\tau},
\qquad v_0,\rho,\varepsilon_\tau>0.
$$

Large flux can therefore create fast channels even when geometric separation is not minimal.

3. **Flux coupling strength.**

<span style="float:right;">(10)</span>

$$
F_{ij}:=|J_{ij}|.
$$

This measures how strongly the two nodes actually exchange coherence.

For algorithms that require affinity-style weights rather than cost-style labels, one may store the monotone transforms

$$
w_{ij}^{(d)}=e^{-\sigma_d \ell_{ij}^{(d)}},\qquad
w_{ij}^{(\tau)}=e^{-\sigma_\tau \tau_{ij}},\qquad
w_{ij}^{(F)}=F_{ij},
$$

with $\sigma_d,\sigma_\tau>0$.

The conceptual separation matters more than the specific transform: **the conductance that drives dynamics is not forced to answer all interpretive questions by itself.**

## Global coherence functional, potential, and flux

The graph functional remains the same in form:

<span style="float:right;">(11)</span>

$$
\mathcal P^{(k)}[C]
=
\frac{\kappa_C}{2}\sum_{(i,j)\in E^{(k)}} w_{ij}^{(k)}\big(C_i^{(k)}-C_j^{(k)}\big)^2
-\sum_{i\in V^{(k)}}V\!\big(C_i^{(k)}\big).
$$

The node potential is

<span style="float:right;">(12)</span>

$$
\Phi_i^{(k)}
=
\kappa_C\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}\big(C_i^{(k)}-C_j^{(k)}\big)
-V'\!\big(C_i^{(k)}\big).
$$

The constitutive flux law is unchanged:

<span style="float:right;">(13)</span>

$$
J_{ij}^{(k)}=-\eta\,w_{ij}^{(k)}\big(\Phi_i^{(k)}-\Phi_j^{(k)}\big).
$$

And the continuity update remains

<span style="float:right;">(14)</span>

$$
\frac{C_i^{(k+1)}-C_i^{(k)}}{\Delta t}
=
-\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)}J_{ij}^{(k)}.
$$

The point of GRC‑v3 is therefore not to replace the loop, but to **enrich the state on which the loop closes**.

## Derived structures – identities, sparks, growth, hierarchy

### Identity basins

GRC‑v3 admits two mutually compatible identification layers.

1. **Flux-topology layer.** As in GRC‑v2, define a directed edge $i\to j$ when $J_{ij}>0$, construct the directed-flux graph, and extract sinks plus their attraction domains.
2. **Geometric layer.** Declare a node to be a basin seed when
   $$
   \|\mathbf g_i\|<\varepsilon_\nabla
   \quad\text{and}\quad
   \mathrm{spec}(H_i)\ \text{matches the chosen stable basin convention}.
   $$

The first layer preserves continuity with GRC‑v2. The second layer restores the direct basin language of continuous RC. In practical implementations one can require agreement between them, or use one as a validation layer for the other.

Appendix B fixes the sign convention more tightly and states the basin and spark criteria in a form that matches the 2025‑11 RC paper more explicitly.

### Spark detection

The continuous RC story identifies sparks with loss of local non-degeneracy. In GRC‑v3 this becomes a direct local test:

<span style="float:right;">(15)</span>

$$
\mathrm{Spark}(i)
\Longleftrightarrow
\|\mathbf g_i\|<\varepsilon_\nabla
\quad\text{and}\quad
\min_{\lambda\in \mathrm{spec}(H_i)} |\lambda| < \varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}.
$$

This is the main conceptual advantage over the v2-only proxy picture. Large graphs may still use a Cheeger-type proxy for speed, but v3 no longer depends on that proxy for the definition itself.

### Soft split with inherited hierarchy

When a spark is triggered at basin-node $i$, GRC‑v3 performs a soft split along the eigen-direction of weakest curvature. The parent node is not removed instantaneously; instead two child nodes are initialized with inherited attributes and diverge over $\tau_{\mathrm{split}}$ steps:

- the parent supplies the coherence budget,
- the children inherit the parent `id` as ancestry and receive new child identities,
- `parent` is set to the original basin,
- and `depth` is incremented by one.

This preserves the smoothness goal of the v2 soft split while making the split semantically about **basin refinement** rather than raw node duplication.

### Birth and pruning

Birth still comes from front propagation. A node with outward flux into inactive neighbourhood can create a new node with initial coherence drawn from that outgoing flux. The difference is that the newborn node is immediately initialized as a basin chart with empty or neutral differential summaries and a fresh identity label.

Pruning still removes non-viable regions, but the redistribution can now be basin-aware rather than uniform over bare vertices. In particular, remaining mass can be sent to parent or sibling basins in proportion to their current effective size $M_i$ or coupling strength $F_{ij}$.

### Abundance

The abundance observables from GRC‑v2 carry over unchanged:

<span style="float:right;">(16)</span>

$$
A^{(k)}=|\mathcal S^{(k)}|,
\qquad
\tilde A^{(k)}=\sum_{s\in\mathcal S^{(k)}} M_s^{\gamma},
\quad \gamma>0,
$$

but GRC‑v3 can additionally record curvature load, hierarchy depth, average gradient magnitude, and multi-metric path statistics.

Appendix A adds the discrete counterparts of **choice**, **collapse**, and the resulting **learning** update, so that the v3 paper tracks more of the phenomenology stated in the 2025‑11 RC basis rather than only identity, spark, and abundance.

## Full closed reflexive loop – algorithmic statement

```text
INPUT: initial graph G0, node coherences {C_i}, parameters
       (α,β,γ,δ,η,κC,λC,ξC,ζC,Δt,ε∇,εspark,εprune,…),
       total budget B = Σ_i C_i(0).

FOR k = 0,1,2,… :

    #--- (a) Basin-attribute summaries from current state ---------------
    for each node i:
        compute gradient summary g_i by Eq.(2)
        compute Hessian summary H_i by Eq.(3)
        compute net flux summary J_i^net by Eq.(4)
        update current basin size / hierarchy labels if tracked incrementally

    #--- (b) Geometry from basin attributes -----------------------------
    for each node i:
        compute node tensor K_i by Eq.(6)

    #--- (c) Edge conductance + analytic labels -------------------------
    for each edge (i,j):
        update base conductance w_ij by Eq.(7)
        compute geometric label ell_d by Eq.(8)
        compute temporal label tau by Eq.(9)
        compute coupling label F by Eq.(10)

    #--- (d) Coherence potential ----------------------------------------
    for each node i:
        Φ_i ← κC * Σ_j w_ij (C_i - C_j) - V'(C_i)      # Eq.(12)

    #--- (e) Flux --------------------------------------------------------
    for each edge (i,j):
        J_ij ← -η * w_ij * (Φ_i - Φ_j)                 # Eq.(13)

    #--- (f) Identity layer ---------------------------------------------
    build directed-flux graph from sign(J_ij)
    identify sinks and attraction basins
    validate or update basin seeds from (||g_i||, spec(H_i))

    #--- (g) Spark detection / soft split -------------------------------
    for each basin-node i:
        if Spark(i) holds by Eq.(15):
            initialise soft split along weakest-curvature direction
            create child ids, parent links, and depth updates

    #--- (h) Front propagation / birth ----------------------------------
    for each active frontier node i:
        compute outward flux F_out
        apply probabilistic or deterministic birth rule
        if birth succeeds:
            create new node j*
            assign C_j* from parent flux budget
            initialize neutral basin attributes for j*

    #--- (i) Pruning / merge-back ---------------------------------------
    for each low-viability node or basin:
        if C_i < εprune and coupling/geometry criteria fail:
            merge to parent or redistribute to siblings/survivors

    #--- (j) Continuity update ------------------------------------------
    for each node i:
        C_i ← C_i - Δt * Σ_j J_ij                       # Eq.(14)

    #--- (k) Exact budget preservation ----------------------------------
    enforce Σ_i C_i = B by exact redistribution / remainder bookkeeping

    #--- (l) Record observables -----------------------------------------
    store A(k), Ã(k), hierarchy depth, gradient norms,
          curvature diagnostics, and multi-metric path statistics

END FOR
```

Every line still depends only on quantities generated by the previous iteration, so the system remains reflexive in exactly the same sense as GRC‑v2:

$$
\text{geometry} \rightarrow \text{potential} \rightarrow \text{flux} \rightarrow \text{coherence} \rightarrow \text{geometry}.
$$

The difference is that the geometry is now represented more faithfully at the node level, and the edge layer is no longer forced into a single overloaded meaning.

## Implementation profile

GRC‑v3 increases state richness but does not change the fundamental computational shape of the model.

| Operation | GRC‑v2 | GRC‑v3 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Conductance update | $O(|E|)$ | $O(|E|)$ |
| Gradient summaries | implicit in flux/Laplacian | $O(|E|)$ |
| Hessian summaries | absent | $O(|E|d^2)$ in local basis dimension $d$ |
| Basin identification | flux-topology only | flux-topology + optional geometric validation |
| Edge interpretation | one weight | one conductance + three analytic labels |

Three remarks matter.

1. **v3 is backward-compatible by disabling features.** Setting the extra summaries to null or not storing the analytic labels reduces the model back toward the v2 regime.
2. **The main overhead is second-order information.** The Hessian summaries are the expensive part; they are also what gives v3 its direct spark condition.
3. **The semantic gain is larger than the numerical overhead in the regimes that care about basin geometry.** If one wants only diffusion on a growing graph, v2 may be enough. If one wants RC-style identity geometry, v3 is the natural discrete object.

## Conclusion

GRC‑v3 closes the main representational gap in GRC‑v2 without breaking the original reflexive loop. The gap was not that v2 failed to evolve coherence on a graph. The gap was that v2 encoded a non-uniform identity basin only indirectly, by distributing one basin across many vertices and then inferring its structure from topology. GRC‑v3 moves the relevant differential information back onto the node where the identity is represented: gradient, Hessian, net flux summary, effective basin size, and hierarchy labels.

At the same time, GRC‑v3 separates three meanings that should not be collapsed:

- conductance for the actual dynamics,
- geometric separation in the induced state-dependent geometry,
- temporal propagation delay,
- and functional coupling strength.

This makes the graph a closer discrete counterpart of the continuous RC theory and of the RC distance construction. A GRC‑v3 node is therefore not just a scalar carrier. It is a compact basin chart inside a closed reflexive graph dynamics.

## Appendix A: Discrete Choice, Collapse, and Learning

The 2025‑11 RC paper treats **choice** and **collapse** as intrinsic consequences of a geometry that can temporarily support multiple compatible attractors. GRC‑v3 can represent the same phenomenon without introducing any new primitive beyond the existing graph state.

### A.1 Choice as a multi-basin compatibility regime

Let $\mathcal S^{(k)}=\{s_1,\dots,s_m\}$ be the sink set at step $k$. For each node $i$ and sink $s_a$, define an attraction score

<span style="float:right;">(A1)</span>

$$
\pi_{i\to s_a}^{(k)}
=
\frac{\exp[-\chi\,D^{(k)}(i,s_a)]}
{\sum_{b=1}^{m}\exp[-\chi\,D^{(k)}(i,s_b)]},
\qquad \chi>0,
$$

where $D^{(k)}(i,s_a)$ is any sink-directed cost compatible with the current state, for example a path cost built from $\Phi$, $\tau$, or a weighted combination of the analytic edge labels. The exact choice of score is constitutive, not axiomatic; what matters is that it is computed from the current reflexive state.

A node $i$ is in a **choice regime** when at least two sinks have non-negligible compatibility:

<span style="float:right;">(A2)</span>

$$
\mathrm{Choice}(i,k)
\Longleftrightarrow
\exists a\neq b \text{ such that }
\pi_{i\to s_a}^{(k)}>\varepsilon_{\mathrm{choice}}
\text{ and }
\pi_{i\to s_b}^{(k)}>\varepsilon_{\mathrm{choice}}.
$$

Thus, a discrete choice is not an extra operator. It is the graph-level situation in which the current geometry and flux topology support more than one viable continuation.

### A.2 Collapse as discrete resolution to one basin

A **collapse** occurs when a previously ambiguous region resolves to one dominant sink. For a node $i$ we say that a collapse to sink $s_\star$ occurs over the interval $[k_-,k_+]$ if

<span style="float:right;">(A3)</span>

$$
\max_a \pi_{i\to s_a}^{(k_-)} < 1-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{coll}}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\pi_{i\to s_\star}^{(k_+)} \ge 1-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{coll}},
$$

with the competing sink scores dropping below $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{coll}}$ at $k_+$. Equivalently, in the hard-routing picture of the directed-flux graph, collapse is the moment at which the successor dynamics becomes unambiguously routed to one sink.

This is the discrete counterpart of the RC statement that a multi-attractor configuration resolves to one stable basin through flux convergence. No extra stochastic decision rule is required.

### A.3 Collapse writes itself into geometry

After collapse, the winning basin receives net coherence and the local differential summaries are updated:

- $C_i$ changes through the continuity step,
- $\mathbf g_i$ and $H_i$ are recomputed from the new neighbourhood state,
- the node tensor $K_i$ and conductances $w_{ij}$ are regenerated,
- and basin assignments become sharper.

This is the discrete counterpart of the RC claim that collapse irreversibly updates geometry. In graph language, the result of collapse is written into the next step's node tensors, conductances, and attraction structure.

### A.4 Learning as persistent post-collapse deformation

GRC‑v3 therefore inherits the RC learning picture directly:

<span style="float:right;">(A4)</span>

$$
\text{perturbation}
\rightarrow
\text{multi-basin compatibility}
\rightarrow
\text{collapse}
\rightarrow
\text{updated }(C,\mathbf g,H,K,w)
\rightarrow
\text{future bias}.
$$

Learning is not a separate memory substrate. It is the persistence of post-collapse geometric deformation in the quantities that govern later dynamics.

## Appendix B: Tightened Basin and Spark Criteria

The main text intentionally left the basin sign convention flexible. To match the 2025‑11 RC basis more tightly, we fix that convention explicitly here.

### B.1 Signed basin Hessian convention

Because discrete second-difference formulas may differ by an overall sign depending on whether one stores curvature of $C$, curvature of a potential derived from $C$, or the negative graph Laplacian convention, define once and for all a global sign $s_H\in\{+1,-1\}$ and the **signed basin Hessian**

<span style="float:right;">(B1)</span>

$$
\widetilde H_i := s_H\,H_i,
$$

with $s_H$ chosen so that stable basin interiors correspond to positive-definite $\widetilde H_i$. After that choice, the basin convention is fixed globally for the entire simulation.

### B.2 Tightened identity-basin seed criterion

A node $i$ is a discrete basin seed when

<span style="float:right;">(B2)</span>

$$
\|\mathbf g_i\| < \varepsilon_\nabla
\quad\text{and}\quad
\lambda_{\min}(\widetilde H_i) > \varepsilon_H > 0.
$$

This is the direct discrete analogue of the RC criterion
$\nabla C = 0$ together with a positive-definite Hessian at a stable attractor.

A full basin is then the attraction domain of such a seed under the directed-flux routing, so the discrete object still satisfies the RC idea that identity is an invariant attractor basin rather than a bare label.

### B.3 Tightened spark criterion

A node $i$ is a spark candidate when it lies at a basin interior but loses one curvature direction:

<span style="float:right;">(B3)</span>

$$
\|\mathbf g_i\| < \varepsilon_\nabla,
\qquad
\lambda_{\min}(\widetilde H_i) < \varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}},
\qquad
\lambda_r(\widetilde H_i) > \varepsilon_H
\ \text{for}\ r=2,\dots,d.
$$

This is the codimension-one discrete version of the RC spark condition: one Hessian eigenvalue approaches zero while the others remain non-degenerate. In determinant form this implies

<span style="float:right;">(B4)</span>

$$
\det(\widetilde H_i)\approx 0
$$

at the transition.

### B.4 Discrete attractor-count change

To match the 2025‑11 statement that a spark changes the number of attractors, GRC‑v3 treats the local degeneracy test as necessary but not sufficient on its own. A completed spark is registered only when the post-event sink count satisfies

<span style="float:right;">(B5)</span>

$$
|\mathcal S^{(k_+)}| = |\mathcal S^{(k_-)}| + 1
$$

or, more generally, when the local basin decomposition gains at least one additional stable child basin after the soft split. This prevents a transient flattening from being misclassified as a true RC spark.

## Appendix C: Discrete Conservation Law as the RC Budget

The 2025‑11 RC basis defines the invariant coherence budget as an integral over the induced volume form. The graph version should be read as a quadrature of that invariant, not as a different principle.

### C.1 Node mass as discrete volume-weighted coherence

Let $\mu_i^{(k)}>0$ be a node measure, interpreted as the graph quadrature weight associated with node $i$. Then define the discrete node mass

<span style="float:right;">(C1)</span>

$$
q_i^{(k)} := \mu_i^{(k)}\,C_i^{(k)}.
$$

The total graph budget is

<span style="float:right;">(C2)</span>

$$
B^{(k)} := \sum_{i\in V^{(k)}} q_i^{(k)}.
$$

This is the discrete analogue of

$$
\int_{\Omega_t} C(x,t)\,dV_g.
$$

### C.2 Unit-measure convention used in the main text

The main text uses the simplest convention $\mu_i^{(k)}\equiv 1$, so that

<span style="float:right;">(C3)</span>

$$
B^{(k)}=\sum_i C_i^{(k)}.
$$

This should be understood as the unit-cell quadrature version of the RC invariant. Equivalently, one may say that the node coherence values in the main text already absorb the local cell measure.

### C.3 Local continuity and global cancellation

With antisymmetric edge fluxes $J_{ij}=-J_{ji}$, a mass-form continuity update reads

<span style="float:right;">(C4)</span>

$$
\frac{q_i^{(k+1)}-q_i^{(k)}}{\Delta t}
=
-\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)}J_{ij}^{(k)}.
$$

Summing over all nodes gives

<span style="float:right;">(C5)</span>

$$
\frac{B^{(k+1)}-B^{(k)}}{\Delta t}
=
-\sum_i\sum_j J_{ij}^{(k)} = 0,
$$

because the edge fluxes cancel pairwise.

Hence the graph budget is conserved exactly between topology events, just as the RC continuum budget is conserved under the continuity law.

### C.4 Topology change and exact budget preservation

During birth, split, merge, or pruning events, GRC‑v3 preserves the same invariant by explicit redistribution of node mass. In other words:

- the continuity step gives conservation on a fixed graph,
- topology events move mass between graph representations,
- and the budget-correction step enforces exact equality of the post-event total with the pre-event total.

Thus the quantity preserved by GRC‑v3 is not merely a bookkeeping sum. It is the discrete quadrature counterpart of the RC global coherence invariant.

## Appendix D: Continuum Correspondence and Known Discretization Gaps

GRC‑v3 is intended as a high-fidelity **discrete realization** of the RC field theory, not as a claim of literal identity with the continuum geometric construction. Relative to the continuous RC basis of the 2025‑11 and 2026‑01 papers, there are several places where the graph model should be read as an approximation, a scope restriction, or a natural extension point rather than a complete equivalence.

### D.1 Boundary geometry versus pruning

In the continuum geometry paper, the support boundary is not modeled as a hard deletion rule. As $C\to 0$ near the edge of the support, the induced geometry can develop an effective large-distance barrier, so that approaching the boundary becomes geometrically costly even before support is lost. In the baseline GRC‑v3 graph construction, by contrast, weak nodes may be removed by pruning and their budget redistributed.

This is a practical discrete rule, but it is not the same mechanism as the continuum boundary-horizon effect. Pruning should therefore be read as a graph-regularization step that preserves the budget and the coarse basin structure, while only approximating the continuum claim that low-coherence boundary regions become hard to traverse before they disappear. A tighter discrete analogue would introduce boundary-barrier or ghost-node constructions rather than relying only on deletion.

### D.2 Intrinsic geometry versus embedding dependence

The continuous RC theory treats space as derived from the support and metric induced by coherence itself. Coordinates are descriptive only. GRC‑v3, however, writes its gradient, Hessian summaries, and geometric edge labels in terms of local edge directions or displacement surrogates. This means that the discrete realization needs some local frame convention.

That convention should not be misunderstood as reintroducing a fixed background space at the level of principle. It is a constitutive choice required to express directional information on a graph. In applications, this local frame may come from a host embedding, from a dynamically induced local basis, or from a purely combinatorial construction. The important limitation is that baseline GRC‑v3 does not by itself prove a unique intrinsic graph analogue of the continuum metric; it specifies one faithful family of approximations.

### D.3 Spatial graph dynamics versus a full Lorentzian layer

The present paper is a discrete theory of RC **spatial slices** with an operational update time. The continuum geometry paper goes further and shows how one may build a Lorentzian spacetime layer with lapse, shift, causal cones, and proper time distinct from spatial distance.

GRC‑v3 does not construct that full discrete spacetime metric. Its temporal-delay labels are operational propagation quantities, not yet a complete graph analogue of Lorentzian causality. This is therefore a scope boundary of the present paper rather than a contradiction: GRC‑v3 captures the reflexive spatial dynamics, while a discrete causal layer remains future work.

### D.4 Global determinism versus local phenomenology of unpredictability

The RC identity-and-choice paper emphasizes that the dynamics may be globally deterministic while remaining locally unpredictable to any observer confined to a partial region of the field. A full simulation of GRC‑v3, however, typically has access to the entire graph state at every step.

For that reason, GRC‑v3 directly reproduces the **global deterministic closure** of RC more faithfully than it reproduces the **phenomenology of local ignorance**. The latter requires additional observer restrictions, finite information horizons, or causal visibility constraints at the runtime level. So the discrete model preserves the structural basis for choice and collapse, but a strong operational analogue of local unpredictability is not supplied by the bare graph equations alone.

### D.5 Identity hierarchy versus fractal scale hierarchy

GRC‑v3 introduces basin attributes, parent relations, and depth labels, so it supports an explicit hierarchy of identities and their descendants. This should not be confused with the Fractal Reflexive Coherence extension, where coherence is itself indexed by a scale coordinate $\sigma$ and multiple scales coexist simultaneously in the field description.

Accordingly, GRC‑v3 models **basin hierarchy**, not a full **scale-resolved coherence field**. A discrete realization of the FRC construction would require nodes to carry multi-scale coherence content or a coupled family of scale-indexed graph states. That extension is compatible with the present framework but is not part of baseline GRC‑v3.

### D.6 Node-level anisotropy versus edge-level isotropy

In the continuum RC theory, anisotropy lives directly in the tensorial geometry, so transport can differ by direction at the same point. In GRC‑v3, anisotropy is represented primarily through node-level differential summaries and the induced node tensor, while the actual dynamic edge conductance remains a scalar per edge.

This means that directional effects are preserved only after projection through the local graph neighborhood and conductance rule. The resulting model is still anisotropic in behavior, but it is not as fine-grained as a representation in which each transport channel carries its own directional constitutive stiffness. In this sense, baseline GRC‑v3 compresses part of the continuum tensor structure into scalar edge transport. The nine-slot G‑RC‑9 family partially addresses this by making directional channels explicit in the substrate.

### D.7 How these gaps should be read

These limitations are not all of the same kind.

- Some are **discretization compromises** internal to baseline GRC‑v3, especially pruning versus boundary barriers and scalar edge conductance versus richer directional transport.
- Some are **constitutive choices** that must be made explicitly in any graph realization, especially the choice of local directional frame.
- Some are **scope boundaries** of the present paper, especially the Lorentzian causal layer and the full FRC scale coordinate.

The claim of GRC‑v3 is therefore precise: it is a discrete reflexive graph theory that preserves the RC loop, identity-basin logic, spark semantics, budget conservation, and enriched basin geometry at high fidelity, while leaving several continuum refinements to future specializations.
