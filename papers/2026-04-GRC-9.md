# Graph‑Reflexive‑Coherence‑9 (G‑RC‑9): Mechanical Nine‑Slot RC with a GRC‑v3 Family Lift

Copyright © 2026 Uroš Jovanovič, CC BY-SA 4.0.

This document presents the **stable G‑RC‑9 specialization** of the Graph‑Reflexive‑Coherence family. Historically the construction was written against the Augmented GRC‑v2 substrate; in this 2026‑04 version the base nine‑slot mechanics are preserved, while **Appendix G** shows how the same family lifts naturally into the **GRC‑v3** framework of basin‑attribute nodes, tightened spark semantics, discrete choice/collapse, and quadrature‑based conservation.

The reflexive dynamical loop is unchanged:

$$
C \;\rightarrow\; K[C] \;\rightarrow\; w[K] \;\rightarrow\; \Phi[w,C] \;\rightarrow\; J[w,\Phi] \;\rightarrow\; \partial_t C + \nabla\!\cdot J = 0 \;\rightarrow\; C.
$$

G‑RC‑9 makes three structural commitments:

1. **Nine‑slot nodes:** each node exposes up to **nine ordered ports** (local capacity / “valence”), organized as a **3×3 edge bundle**.
2. **Mechanical sparks:** a spark refines the substrate by **expanding** a saturated sink into a small connected module (new nodes are mechanical, not just semantic).
3. **Invertible column coarse‑graining:** a deterministic, local operator $\mathcal G$ pools columns while retaining intra‑column mode profiles; **Split** reconstructs the nine‑slot state exactly.

## Abstract

We define **Graph‑Reflexive‑Coherence on nine‑slot nodes (G‑RC‑9)**: a reflexive, budget‑conserving coherence dynamics on a **port‑labeled graph** in which every node exposes up to nine ordered incident ports. The ports are organized as a **3×3 bundle** with three *mode rows* (triplet directions) and three *polarity columns* $(1,4,7)$, $(2,5,8)$, $(3,6,9)$. This factorization supplies (i) a canonical local basis for geometry in the coherence tensor, (ii) deterministic **mechanical** topology refinement via spark expansion modules, and (iii) a lossless local multiscale interface through invertible column coarse‑graining $\mathcal G$ and Split. All dynamical equations (metric generation, potential, flux, continuity, and budget preservation) follow the unchanged reflexive loop of G‑RC; **Appendix G** states the explicit lift from the original GRC‑v2 reading of this family to the richer GRC‑v3 reading.

## 1. Core objects — the static substrate

### 1.1 Port‑labeled graph substrate

A G‑RC‑9 state lives on a **port‑graph**
$$
\Gamma^{(k)}=(V^{(k)},E^{(k)},\sigma^{(k)}),
$$
where each edge $e\in E^{(k)}$ connects two endpoint ports:
$$
\sigma^{(k)}(e)=\big((i,r),(j,s)\big),\qquad r,s\in\{1,\dots,9\}.
$$

An **inactive port** is an unused slot. The **active degree** of a node is
$$
\deg_{\mathrm{act}}(i)=\#\{\, r\in\{1,\dots,9\}\mid \exists e:\ \sigma(e)=((i,r),(\cdot,\cdot))\,\}\ \le 9.
$$

We assume the **underlying adjacency is undirected**, and flux is an oriented quantity on each undirected edge $\{i,j\}$ with an arbitrary orientation convention (so antisymmetry is meaningful).

**Lookup rule (ports → edge scalars).** Implementations may store edge‑attached quantities either:

- **per undirected edge** $\{i,j\}$ (a single $w_{ij}$ and oriented $J_{ij}=-J_{ji}$), or
- **per occupied port** $(i,a,b)\to j$ (a value $w_{i,a,b}$ attached to the slot that realizes the adjacency).

Throughout this document, whenever we write $w_{ij}$ or $J_{ij}$ we mean the scalar value associated with the unique active port‑pair connecting $i$ and $j$ (if multiple edges are allowed, aggregate them explicitly). If an edge is stored as a port‑pair $\sigma(e)=((i,r),(j,s))$, unpack $r\leftrightarrow(a,b)$ and interpret
$$
w_{ij}\equiv w_{i,a,b},\qquad J_{ij}\equiv J_{i,a,b},
$$
with the understanding that $w_{ij}=w_{ji}$ while $J_{ji}=-J_{ij}$.

### 1.2 The 3×3 port bundle (ordered slots)

Each port index $r\in\{1,\dots,9\}$ is identified with a matrix coordinate $(a,b)$:
$$
a\in\{1,2,3\}\ \text{(mode row)},\qquad b\in\{1,2,3\}\ \text{(polarity column)},\qquad r=b+3(a-1).
$$

Canonical partitions:

- **Mode rows (triplets):** $\{1,2,3\}$, $\{4,5,6\}$, $\{7,8,9\}$.
- **Polarity columns:** $C_1=\{1,4,7\}$, $C_2=\{2,5,8\}$, $C_3=\{3,6,9\}$.

For node $i$, let $n(i,a,b)$ denote the neighbor reached from $i$ via the (active) port $(a,b)$, if such an edge exists.

#### What the 3×3 bundle *means* (rows vs columns)

GRC-9 assigns each node up to nine ordered incident ports and indexes them as a 3×3 bundle $(a,b)$. This is not an extra field; it is a **fixed local coordinate convention** that factorizes the node’s incident degrees of freedom into two orthogonal roles:

1. **Mode rows** $a\in{1,2,3}$ are the node’s **local geometric directions**.
   They are the discrete analogue of choosing a minimal local frame in which “directional mismatch” can be expressed. In the original Reflexive Coherence model, geometry reacts to local structure in $C$ (and its induced flows) through tensorial quantities: one needs a notion of **direction** to speak about anisotropy and curvature feedback. In GRC-9, that directional resolution is provided by the three rows: each row collects a triplet of incident edges that are treated as belonging to the same local direction. This is why the row index appears explicitly in the coherence tensor $K_i$: the gradient/mismatch contribution is accumulated **row-wise**, producing an anisotropic local tensor in the canonical basis ${\mathbf e_a}$.

2. **Polarity columns** $b\in{1,2,3}$ are the node’s **interface families** (boundary channels).
   They do *not* define new geometric directions; instead they define a deterministic partition of the node’s boundary into three stable families that can be preserved across topology events and across scale. In the continuous RC story, a spark indicates a local “chart failure” (loss of local convexity / Hessian degeneracy): the system must refine its representation without appealing to external labels. Columns serve exactly this role in GRC-9. They make spark responses **mechanical and deterministic**: when a sink expands, its incident edges are reassigned by column family, and when we form a multiscale view we pool quantities by column using $\mathcal G$ (and recover exactly via Split). In this sense, columns provide the discrete handle for “interface” and “scale” that is implicit in the continuous model.

Put differently: **rows are the minimal directional basis for geometry**, while **columns are the stable boundary partition that supports refinement and multiscale structure**.

#### Operational mapping to RC primitives (what uses rows, what uses columns)

| Bundle index | RC interpretation  | What it controls in GRC-9 | Where it appears |
| ------- | ------- | ------- | ------- |
| **Row $a$** (mode)  | Local directional structure (a minimal frame for anisotropic mismatch / curvature response)   | How coherence differences contribute to the tensor and hence to the induced geometry   | $K_i$ gradient term is accumulated row-wise   |
| **Column $b$** (polarity) | Boundary/interface partition; a discrete “chart handle” that remains meaningful under refinement | Deterministic routing of boundary edges under spark expansion; deterministic coarse-graining/scale ladder | Column curvature proxy $H^{(b)}$; rewiring by column; $\mathcal G$/Split pools by column |

#### One intuitive picture (without changing the math)

You can think of each node as having **three “directions”** (rows) along which it measures and stores geometric stress, and **three “channels”** (columns) through which it connects to its environment in a way that remains consistent under growth and refinement. The same physical edges participate in both views: every incident edge belongs to exactly one row and one column. The reason for introducing both is that RC needs both capabilities:

* a directional basis (to make geometry meaningful), and
* a stable interface partition (to make sparks and scale transitions deterministic).

This 3×3 factorization is the smallest choice that supplies both without introducing extra primitive fields.

### 1.3 Fields, parameters, invariant

| Object | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $C_i^{(k)}\ge 0$ | Coherence density on node $i$. |
| $w_{ij}^{(k)}>0$ | Symmetric edge conductance (metric surrogate) on $\{i,j\}$. |
| $J_{ij}^{(k)}\in\mathbb R$ | Oriented flux on $\{i,j\}$ (antisymmetric: $J_{ij}=-J_{ji}$). |
| $\Phi_i^{(k)}$ | Coherence potential (discrete variational derivative). |
| $B=\sum_{i\in V^{(0)}} C_i^{(0)}$ | Global coherence budget (exact invariant). |

## 2. Geometry from coherence — the coherence tensor

For each node $i$ and mode row $a$, define the set of incident edges occupying that row

$$
\mathcal T_i^{(a)}:=\{\, (i,j)\in E\mid (i,\cdot)\text{ uses a port in row }a\,\}.
$$

Let $\mathbf e_a$ be the canonical basis vectors for rows $a=1,2,3$. The **local coherence tensor** is

$$
K_i^{(k)}=
\lambda_C\,C_i^{(k)}\,I
+\xi_C\sum_{a=1}^{3}\Biggl(\sum_{j:\ (i,j)\in \mathcal T_i^{(a)}} w_{ij}^{(k)}\bigl(C_{j}^{(k)}-C_i^{(k)}\bigr)^2\Biggr)\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a
+\zeta_C\Bigl(\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)}J_{ij}^{(k)}\Bigr)^2 I.
\tag{1}
$$

The second term uses the dyadic product $\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a$, which (for an orthonormal basis $\{\mathbf e_a\}_{a=1}^3)$ is the **rank-1 projector** onto the $a$-th row direction. The identity operator on this three-dimensional row-space is

$$
I=\sum_{a=1}^{3}\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a =
\begin{pmatrix}
  1&0&0\\
  0&1&0\\
  0&0&1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Terms proportional to $I$ contribute **isotropically** to $K_i$, while the row-wise mismatch term builds an **anisotropic** tensor by assigning potentially different scalar weights to each projector $\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a$. Intuitively, $I$ shifts the tensor equally in all three row directions, whereas $\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a$ lets the $a$-th direction stiffen or soften independently.

> In G‑RC‑9, the triplet directions are not heuristic groupings: they are the fixed mode rows of the port bundle.

**Row vs column semantics (implementation contract).**

| Index | Name | What it is used for (canonically) |
|---|---|---|
| $a\in\{1,2,3\}$ | **Mode row** | The three *triplet directions* that appear explicitly in the gradient term of $K_i$ (Eq. (1)). Row‑wise grouping defines the local basis $\{\mathbf e_a\}$. |
| $b\in\{1,2,3\}$ | **Polarity column** | The three *column families* used for deterministic coarse‑graining $\mathcal G$/Split (Section 9) and for the column curvature diagnostic $H_i^{(b)}$ (Eq. (11)). |
| $r\in\{1,\dots,9\}$ | **Port id** | A fixed ordered slot; mapped to $(a,b)$ by $r=b+3(a-1)$. |

The model is consistent if **row‑operations never depend on column labels** (unless explicitly stated), and **column‑pooling never mixes rows** (it preserves the intra‑column row profile).

## 3. Metric generation — edge conductances

Edge conductances are produced by the same exponential metric map as in GRC‑v2. In the GRC‑v3 reading of this paper, this quantity is interpreted specifically as the **base conductance** that drives the dynamics; additional analytic labels can be stored alongside it without changing the equations here (see Appendix G).

$$
w_{ij}^{(k+1)}=
\exp\!\Bigl[
-\alpha\,\tfrac{C_i^{(k)}+C_j^{(k)}}{2}
-\beta\,\tfrac{(C_i^{(k)}-C_j^{(k)})^2}{2}
-\gamma\,\tfrac{(J_{ij}^{(k)})^2}{2}
-\delta\,\kappa^{\mathrm{Ric}}_{ij}
\Bigr].
\tag{2}
$$

**Curvature term.** A concrete triangle‑free choice $\kappa^{\mathrm{Ric}}_{ij}=\kappa^{\mathrm{OR}}_{ij}$ (Ollivier–Ricci) is defined in **Appendix A**. If the substrate enforces row‑triangles, triangle‑based curvatures may be substituted; G‑RC‑9 does not require that structure.

Parameter‑scale guidance for $\delta$ and $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}$ is given in **Appendix D** (calibration procedure).

## 4. Coherence functional and node potential

Define the coherence functional

$$
\mathcal P^{(k)}[C]=
\frac{\kappa_C}{2}\sum_{\{i,j\}\in E^{(k)}} w_{ij}^{(k)}\,(C_i^{(k)}-C_j^{(k)})^2
-\sum_{i\in V^{(k)}} V\!\bigl(C_i^{(k)}\bigr),
\tag{3}
$$
and its variational derivative (coherence potential)

$$
\Phi_i^{(k)}=
\kappa_C\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ij}^{(k)}\,(C_i^{(k)}-C_j^{(k)})
- V'\!\bigl(C_i^{(k)}\bigr).
\tag{4}
$$

## 5. Constitutive law — flux from geometry

Flux follows the gradient‑flow law

$$
J_{ij}^{(k)}=-\eta\, w_{ij}^{(k)}\bigl(\Phi_i^{(k)}-\Phi_j^{(k)}\bigr),
\qquad J_{ji}^{(k)}=-J_{ij}^{(k)}.
\tag{5}
$$

## 6. Discrete continuity (mass balance)

Local conservation is

$$
C_i^{(k+1)}=C_i^{(k)}-\Delta t\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} J_{ij}^{(k)}.
\tag{6}
$$

## 7. Global invariance (exact budget preservation)

Let $B=\sum_i C_i^{(0)}$. Enforce

$$
\sum_{i\in V^{(k)}} C_i^{(k)}=B
\tag{7}
$$

after topology changes (and optionally every step).

### 7.1 Uniform shift (simple)

$$
\delta=\frac{B-\sum_i C_i^{(k)}}{|V^{(k)}|},\qquad C_i^{(k)}\leftarrow C_i^{(k)}+\delta.
$$

### 7.2 Positivity‑preserving correction (recommended)

If a uniform shift would make some $C_i$ negative, project $\mathbf C$ onto the nonnegative simplex with the correct total

$$
\Delta_B:=\{x\in\mathbb R^{|V|}_{\ge 0}: \sum_i x_i=B\},\qquad
\mathbf C\leftarrow \mathrm{Proj}_{\Delta_B}(\mathbf C).
$$

### 7.3 Default workflow

A practical default is:

1. Apply the **uniform shift** each step (cheap).
2. If any $C_i$ becomes negative, immediately apply the **simplex projection** for that step only.

This keeps the common case fast while preventing silent violations of the state space $C_i\ge 0$.

## 8. Derived structures — identities, sparks, growth, abundance

### 8.1 Directed‑flux graph, sinks, identity basins

Define the **successor map**

$$
f(i)=
\begin{cases}
\arg\max_{j\in\mathcal N(i)} J_{ij} & \text{if }\max_{j}J_{ij}>0,\\
i & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\tag{8}
$$

A node $s$ is a **sink** if it has no positive outgoing flux and receives positive inflow from at least one neighbor

$$
s\in S \iff \max_{j\in\mathcal N(s)} J_{sj}\le 0\ \ \text{and}\ \ \exists j\in\mathcal N(s):\ J_{js}>0.
\tag{9}
$$

The **identity basin** of a sink $s$ is

$$
B_s:=\{\, i\in V\mid f^{m}(i)=s\ \text{for some }m\ge 0\,\}.
\tag{10}
$$

> If a node has a unique strictly positive outgoing flux, the above reduces to the “follow the unique positive outflow” successor rule. When multiple outflows compete or ties occur, Eq. (8) fixes a deterministic partition by $\arg\max$.

### 8.2 Spark detection (RC‑compatible trigger)

A spark is the discrete analogue of “local loss of convexity / chart failure” in RC. In G‑RC‑9, chart failure becomes consequential when local degrees of freedom saturate.

**Eligibility (valence saturation)**

$$
\deg_{\mathrm{act}}(s)=9.
$$

*Optional relaxation (near‑saturation).* If a given application deliberately leaves one port inactive for future growth, one may use $\deg_{\mathrm{act}}(s)\ge 8$ and treat missing ports as **virtual zero‑conductance stubs** when computing the column diagnostic below. This changes event gating and should be regarded as an extension; the canonical G‑RC‑9 rule uses full saturation $=9$.

**Instability test:** reuse the local instability proxy from GRC‑v2 (e.g. a local cut / conductance proxy on $\{s\}\cup\mathcal N(s)$). Denote it $\mathrm{Instability}(s)$. Appendix G explains how to tighten this trigger in the GRC‑v3 family by reading the same event as basin‑attribute degeneracy plus post‑event attractor-count change.

**Column diagnostic (fast, local)**

$$
H_s^{(b)}:=\sum_{a=1}^{3}\, w_{s,a,b}\,\bigl(C_{\,n(s,a,b)}-C_s\bigr),\qquad b=1,2,3,
\tag{11}
$$
where $w_{s,a,b}$ is the conductance on the edge occupying port $(a,b)$ (if active; otherwise treat as $0$ in the optional near‑saturation extension).

Trigger

$$
\mathrm{Spark}(s):=\bigl(\deg_{\mathrm{act}}(s)=9\bigr)\ \wedge\ \Bigl(\mathrm{Instability}(s)\ \ \text{or}\ \ \min_b|H_s^{(b)}|<\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}\Bigr).
\tag{12}
$$

*Optional signed criterion.* In addition to the small‑magnitude test, one may trigger when any column curvature proxy changes sign between steps

$$
H_s^{(b)}(k)\,H_s^{(b)}(k-1)<0,
$$
which is a closer discrete analogue of an eigenvalue crossing through zero. This requires storing the previous $H_s^{(b)}$ values.

### 8.3 Spark expansion (mechanical topology refinement)

When $\mathrm{Spark}(s)$ holds at a sink $s$, G‑RC‑9 performs **local refinement**: replace $s$ by a connected module whose boundary provides additional ports for future growth.

#### 8.3.1 Expansion size from target effective degree

Let $D_{\mathrm{eff}}(s)$ be the desired external port capacity of the refined identity (a policy choice). For a connected module with $n$ nine‑slot nodes and **tree** internal wiring (minimal internal edges), the maximum boundary capacity is

$$
D_{\mathrm{ext,max}}(n)=9n-2(n-1)=7n+2.
$$

Choose the minimal

$$
n=\left\lceil \frac{D_{\mathrm{eff}}(s)-2}{7}\right\rceil.
\tag{13}
$$

#### 8.3.2 Canonical column‑preserving module (core + 3 primary satellites)

Create a **core** node $c$ and three **primary satellites** $s_1,s_2,s_3$ (one per column). Connect them with three internal edges using fixed “center‑of‑column” ports on the core

$$
(c,2)\!\leftrightarrow\!(s_1,5),\quad (c,5)\!\leftrightarrow\!(s_2,5),\quad (c,8)\!\leftrightarrow\!(s_3,5).
\tag{14}
$$

If $n>4$, attach additional nodes as a tree under one of the three satellites (round‑robin by column), always using port 5 on the new node and the lowest‑indexed available port on the existing column tree node.

**Remark (why the spine uses column 2 and port 5).** The canonical expansion module chooses the core ports $\{2,5,8\}$ (the three rows of column 2) and uses port 5 on each satellite as the attachment point. This is a **pure convention** that buys determinism and symmetry: port 5 is the unique geometric center of the 3×3 bundle, so “attach through the center” is a rule that requires no case distinctions, and confining the internal spine to a single column keeps the other two columns maximally available for outward growth and future boundary reassignment. For example, using $\{3,6,9\}$ (column 3) would be mathematically admissible, but it would lose the distinguished central anchor (port 5) and therefore requires additional tie-breaking conventions to remain equally canonical. The choice does not affect the core dynamics (Eqs. (1)–(7)); it only fixes a reproducible mechanical wiring pattern for sparks.

#### 8.3.3 Reassignment of the old boundary (local, deterministic)

Node $s$ has up to nine incident edges, each occupying a port $r\in\{1,\dots,9\}$. Reassign every old incident edge by its column family:

If the old endpoint was $(s,r)$ with $r\in C_b$, redirect it to the corresponding port on the primary satellite $s_b$

$$
((s,r),(j,t))\ \mapsto\ ((s_b,r),(j,t)),\qquad b=\mathrm{column}(r).
\tag{15}
$$

Any new boundary capacity created by the module remains inactive and is filled later by front growth.

#### 8.3.4 State transfer and invariants

Let the pre‑spark coherence be $C_s$. Transfer it to the primary satellites (core optional)

$$
C_{s_1}=p_1 C_s,\quad C_{s_2}=p_2 C_s,\quad C_{s_3}=p_3 C_s,\quad C_c=0,\qquad p_1+p_2+p_3=1,
\tag{16}
$$

with default $p_b=\tfrac13$, or $p_b$ proportional to the column inflow magnitudes at $s$. Initialize new internal edge conductances either by a fixed bond value $w_{\mathrm{bond}}$ or by a local aggregate of the old column conductances (e.g. geometric mean).

Budget preservation (Section 7) must be enforced immediately after expansion (and at the end of each timestep).

#### 8.3.5 Optional gradualization (adiabatic expansion)

If an instantaneous expansion causes undesirable transients, implement a short expansion schedule of $\tau_{\mathrm{exp}}$ substeps (typical $\tau_{\mathrm{exp}}\in[5,20]$) during which

- boundary reassignment is phased in with a mixing factor $\theta\in[0,1]$,
- internal bond weights ramp from $0$ to $w_{\mathrm{bond}}$.

### 8.4 Seed/front propagation (growth)

Front growth is unchanged in principle; the nine‑slot axiom makes port selection deterministic. When a new node is created, it occupies the **lowest‑indexed inactive port** of the parent. A typical birth rule uses outward flux pressure

$$
p_{\mathrm{birth}}(i)=1-\exp(-\lambda\,F_i^{\mathrm{out}}).
$$

### 8.5 Abundance

Abundance may be measured as the number of sinks,

$$
A^{(k)}=|S^{(k)}|,
$$

and, when basin sizes matter, by a **scale‑weighted abundance**

$$
\tilde A^{(k)}=\sum_{s\in S^{(k)}} |B_s|^{\gamma},
\qquad \gamma>0.
\tag{17}
$$

Values $\gamma>1$ emphasize large basins, while $\gamma<1$ emphasizes proliferation of many small basins.

## 9. Invertible column coarse‑graining $\mathcal G$ and Split

Let $X_{i,a,b}\ge 0$ be any **nonnegative port‑attached scalar field** (conductance, absolute flux magnitude, curvature proxy, etc.).

**Column total**

$$
\bar X_{i,b}:=\sum_{a=1}^{3} X_{i,a,b}.
\tag{18}
$$

**Intra‑column mode profile**

$$
\pi^X_{i,a\mid b}:=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{X_{i,a,b}}{\bar X_{i,b}}, & \bar X_{i,b}>0,\\
\dfrac13, & \bar X_{i,b}=0,
\end{cases}
\qquad \sum_{a=1}^{3}\pi^X_{i,a\mid b}=1.
\tag{19}
$$

**Definition (lossless column coarse‑graining).**

$$
\mathcal G(X):=\bigl(\bar X,\pi^X\bigr).
\tag{20}
$$

**Definition (Split / exact refinement).**

$$
\mathrm{Split}(\bar X,\pi^X)_{i,a,b}:=\pi^X_{i,a\mid b}\,\bar X_{i,b}.
\tag{21}
$$

**Invertibility (by construction).**

$$
\mathrm{Split}\circ \mathcal G=\mathrm{Id}\ \text{on nonnegative }X,\qquad \mathcal G\circ \mathrm{Split}=\mathrm{Id}\ \text{on admissible }(\bar X,\pi).
\tag{22}
$$

**Lemma 1 (Invertibility of $\mathcal G$ and Split).** For any non‑negative port field $X$, the composition $\mathrm{Split}(\mathcal G(X))=X$. Conversely, for any admissible pair $(\bar X,\pi)$ with $\bar X_{i,b}\ge 0$ and $\pi_{i,\cdot\mid b}\in\Delta^2$, we have $\mathcal G(\mathrm{Split}(\bar X,\pi))=(\bar X,\pi)$.

*Proof.* Immediate from definitions: $\bar X_{i,b}=\sum_a X_{i,a,b}$ and $\pi_{i,a\mid b}=X_{i,a,b}/\bar X_{i,b}$ (or uniform when $\bar X_{i,b}=0$). Substituting into Split gives $X_{i,a,b}$, while applying $\mathcal G$ to Split re‑sums the reconstructed column totals and re‑normalizes the same profiles. *QED*

### 9.1 Signed flux coarse‑graining (two options)

Flux is signed, so an exact lossless encoding needs one extra degree of freedom beyond a single nonnegative simplex.

- **Lossless (recommended when exactness matters).** Decompose
  $$
  J_{i,a,b}=J^+_{i,a,b}-J^-_{i,a,b},\qquad
  J^+_{i,a,b}:=\max(J_{i,a,b},0),\quad
  J^-_{i,a,b}:=\max(-J_{i,a,b},0),
  $$
  
  and apply $\mathcal G$/Split to $J^+$ and $J^-$ separately (both are nonnegative). This preserves the full signed nine‑slot field exactly.

- **Compressed (useful for diagnostics / low memory).** Store the signed column total
  $\bar J_{i,b}:=\sum_a J_{i,a,b}$ and the normalized absolute profile
  $$
  \pi^{|J|}_{i,a\mid b}:=
  \frac{|J_{i,a,b}|}{\sum_{a'}|J_{i,a',b}|}
  \quad(\text{uniform if denominator }0).
  $$
  
  A canonical reconstruction rule is
  $$
  J_{i,a,b}\approx s_{i,b}\,\pi^{|J|}_{i,a\mid b}\,|\bar J_{i,b}|,\qquad s_{i,b}:=\operatorname{sign}(\bar J_{i,b}).
  $$
  
  If $\bar J_{i,b}=0$ set $s_{i,b}=0$ (so the reconstructed column is identically zero). This representation is **not strictly lossless** when signs vary within a column; use the $J^+,J^-$ encoding above for exactness.

## 10. Multiscale usage note (caching and hierarchy extraction)

$\mathcal G$ is deterministic and local, so it may be computed **on demand** at any step.

If you cache coarse states $(\bar X,\pi)$ across steps, refresh them whenever topology events rewire ports (growth or expansion) or whenever the underlying $X$ values change. Practical caching and hierarchy‑extraction procedures are given in **Appendix D**.

## 11. Full closed loop — algorithmic statement

```
INPUT: Port-graph Γ^(0) with up to 9 ordered ports per node,
       initial coherence C_i ≥ 0, budget B = Σ_i C_i,
       parameters (λ_C,ξ_C,ζ_C, α,β,γ,δ, κ_C, η, ),
       spark thresholds, and an expansion policy for D_eff.

FOR k = 0,1,2,...:(a) Geometry:
      compute K_i via (1).

  (b) Metric update:
      update w_ij via (2).

  (c) Potential:
      compute Φ_i via (4).

  (d) Flux:
      compute J_ij via (5).

  (e) Basin / sink structure:
      compute f, sinks S, basins B_s via (8)–(10).

  (f) Spark detection and expansion:
      for each sink s:
          if Spark(s) via (12):
              choose D_eff(s); execute Expand(s; D_eff) per §8.3.
              enforce budget preservation immediately (Section 7).

  (g) Growth (optional):
      apply front/seed rules; fill lowest inactive ports first.

  (h) Continuity:
      update C via (6).

  (i) Budget preservation:
      enforce (7) using uniform shift or simplex projection.

END FOR
```

## Appendix A. A concrete curvature choice (Ollivier–Ricci, triangle‑free)

To make Eq. (2) fully specified **without requiring triangles**, one concrete option is **Ollivier–Ricci curvature** on weighted graphs. (If the substrate enforces row‑triangles, triangle‑based curvatures may be substituted; Ollivier–Ricci does not require that structure.)

Let $d(i,j)$ be the shortest‑path distance using edge lengths $\ell_{uv}:=1/w_{uv}$ (or any monotone choice). For each node $i$, define a probability measure $m_i$ on its neighbors

$$
m_i(j)=\frac{w_{ij}}{\sum_{k\in\mathcal N(i)} w_{ik}},\qquad j\in\mathcal N(i).
$$

Let $W_1(m_i,m_j)$ be the 1‑Wasserstein distance between neighbor measures using ground metric $d(\cdot,\cdot)$. Define

$$
\kappa^{\mathrm{OR}}_{ij}=1-\frac{W_1(m_i,m_j)}{d(i,j)},
$$

and set $\kappa^{\mathrm{Ric}}_{ij}:=\kappa^{\mathrm{OR}}_{ij}$ in Eq. (2), optionally rescaled/clipped for numerical stability.

> Implementation note: exact $W_1$ can be solved by a small linear program on supports $\mathcal N(i)\cup \mathcal N(j)$; fast approximations (entropic regularization) are also common.

## Appendix B. Interpretation note (“atoms → molecules”)

## Appendix C. Why nine ports? (Why $3\times3$ is the canonical G‑RC choice)

G‑RC‑9 can be generalized to $N^2$ ports (an $N\times N$ bundle), but the choice $N=3$ is singled out because it is the **smallest square** that preserves the semantics already present in the RC/G‑RC construction while keeping topology events deterministic and analytically tractable.

### C.1 The triplet is already intrinsic to the RC tensor structure

The G‑RC coherence tensor $K$ is built from three conceptually distinct contributions (density‑like, gradient‑like, flux‑like). In G‑RC‑9 this maps to **three independent local mode directions** (the three rows of the port bundle). Choosing $N=3$ introduces **no new primitive axes**: it makes explicit a triplet that already exists in the underlying theory.

By contrast, $N=4$ (a 4×4 port bundle) implicitly asserts **four** fundamental local mode directions. That may be a valid extension, but it is no longer a minimal specialization of G‑RC; it changes the theory’s primitive arity.

### C.2 $3\times3$ is the smallest square that supports a canonical “center” for deterministic wiring

A central technical requirement is that topology change (spark expansion) is **mechanical** and **deterministic**, not dependent on ad‑hoc edge selection.

A 3×3 bundle has a unique center port (index 5) and a unique “center of each column” (ports 2, 5, 8). This yields canonical, symmetry‑respecting wiring for expansion modules (Section 8.3) without extra conventions. Even‑$N$ bundles lack a unique center (they have a 2×2 center block), so canonical wiring requires additional symmetry‑breaking rules.

### C.3 Minimal “mechanical refinement” capacity

With $N^2$ ports per node, a satellite uses one internal port to connect to its parent and can expose at most $N^2-1$ external ports. A star with $k$ satellites therefore has capacity

$$
D_{\mathrm{ext}}\approx k\,(N^2-1).
$$

For $N=3$, the increment is $8$ per satellite, which gives **smooth capacity growth** under repeated sparks (arbitrary effective degrees can be realized without very large jumps). For $N=4$, the increment is $15$ per satellite, making capacity changes coarser. For $N=2$, the increment $3$ is often too small to support rich local geometry under the same dynamics.

### C.4 Branching factor and multiscale structure

The column coarse‑graining $\mathcal G$ has branching factor equal to the number of columns: **$N$**. Choosing $N$ therefore fixes the fundamental arity of the multiscale ladder. $N=3$ yields a 3‑branch cascade consistent with the “triplet‑of‑triplets” interpretation (mode triplet × polarity triplet). Other $N$ produce different hierarchy arities and should be treated as extensions.

### C.5 Why not just allow large degree directly (as in GRC‑v2)?

GRC‑v2 can represent high degree by allowing a single node to have many incident edges. G‑RC‑9 instead enforces a local maximum degree (valence) and realizes high effective degree via **modules**.

This constraint is the mechanism that makes sparks RC‑compatible in a discrete substrate: a spark indicates “local chart failure”; the discrete remedy is “local refinement” (create more nodes). Choosing 9 makes the cap high enough to support nontrivial local geometry before refinement, but low enough that refinement occurs in interesting regimes.

### C.6 Summary

Nine ports are “best” in the sense of being **minimal, canonical, and structurally aligned**:

1. It matches the intrinsic triplet structure already present in RC/G‑RC (no new primitive axes).
2. It provides a unique center for deterministic expansion wiring.
3. It yields smooth capacity increments under mechanical refinement.
4. It induces a 3‑branch multiscale ladder consistent with the intended fractal/cascade interpretation.

Other squares $N^2$ are possible, but they should be treated as genuine extensions rather than minimal specializations.

## Appendix D. Practical notes (parameters, caching, and hierarchy extraction)

### D.1 Parameter scale guidance (heuristics)

The curvature coupling $\delta$ in Eq. (2) should typically be small enough that metric feedback does not dominate density/gradient terms, but large enough to shape geometry in bottleneck regions. A robust way to set $\delta$ and $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}$ is **relative calibration**:

1. Run the system for a short burn‑in window (without sparks) and record typical magnitudes:
   - $M_H := \mathrm{median}_i\,\mathrm{median}_b\,|H_i^{(b)}|$,
   - $M_C := \mathrm{median}_{\{i,j\}}\,|C_i-C_j|$.
2. Choose $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}$ as a small fraction of $M_H$ (e.g. $10^{-2}M_H$ to $10^{-1}M_H$), then adjust to hit a desired spark rate.
3. Choose $\delta$ so the curvature contribution in the exponent of Eq. (2) is typically smaller than the density term: a common starting point is $\delta\in[0.01\alpha,\ 0.2\alpha]$, then tune while monitoring whether curvature overwhelms gradient‑driven flux.

These are heuristics; the reliable procedure is to monitor (i) spark rate, (ii) distribution of $w$, and (iii) whether flux collapses into frozen bottlenecks.

### D.2 Coarse‑state caching

If you cache $(\bar X,\pi)$ across iterations, refresh it whenever a topology event rewires ports (growth or expansion). A safe default is to compute $\mathcal G$ **on demand** whenever a multiscale view is requested.

### D.3 Storage note: profile sparsity

In many runs the intra‑column profiles $\pi_{i,\cdot\mid b}$ become close to one‑hot (one mode dominates a column). When memory is critical, store $\pi$ in a compressed form (e.g. dominant index + residual) while keeping enough information to reconstruct exactly (lossless) or approximately (diagnostic), depending on the chosen encoding.

### D.4 Extracting a multiscale identity tree (visualization)

A simple visualization pipeline that produces an explicit **ternary tree** of identities is:

1. Compute sinks $S$ and basins $B_s$ on the fine graph.
2. For each sink (or sink‑module) define three **column‑restricted subgraphs** by retaining only boundary edges whose local port at the sink‑module lies in column $C_b$, $b=1,2,3$. Recompute sinks/basins in each restricted subgraph to obtain “children identities”.
3. Repeat recursively inside each child until no further nontrivial subdivision occurs (or until a depth limit is reached).

This produces a tree where each identity can have up to three children corresponding to the three polarity columns. It is consistent with the role of columns as the canonical coarse‑graining families in $\mathcal G$.

> Note: this is a visualization/extraction procedure. The dynamical model itself does not require committing to a single coarse‑graph construction unless one intends to simulate directly on coarse levels.

## Appendix E. Worked example: cell division as spark-driven identity fission (two sinks)

This appendix illustrates how **a single pre-spark identity** can yield **two post-spark identities** under the canonical G-RC-9 “mechanical expansion” operator (Section 8.3). The key point is that **G-RC-9 does not split a sink into two sinks directly**; instead, it refines the substrate, after which the reflexive flow can reorganize into **two stable sinks** (two basins) — i.e. an identity fission analogous to cell division.

### E.1 Setup: one identity (one sink with a large basin)

Let $s$ be a sink at step $k$, with basin $B_s$ (Eq. (10)). Assume $s$ is near saturation:

* $\deg_{\mathrm{act}}s=9$,
* $\mathrm{Spark}\ s$ holds by Eq. (12) (instability and/or $\min_b |H_s^{(b)}|<\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}}$).

Interpretation: the identity centered on $s$ has reached a regime where its local chart is no longer stable (a discrete analogue of Hessian degeneracy), so refinement is triggered.

### E.2 Canonical spark action: expand $s$ into a module

Apply **Expand** (Section 8.3). Replace $s$ by a **core + three primary satellites** module:

* core node $c$,
* satellites $s_1,s_2,s_3$ corresponding to columns $C_1,C_2,C_3$,
* connect them by the canonical column-preserving internal edges (Eq. (14)),
* reattach the old boundary edges by column (Eq. (15)).

Transfer coherence:
$$
C_{s_1}=p_1 C_s,\quad C_{s_2}=p_2 C_s,\quad C_{s_3}=p_3 C_s,\quad C_c=0,\quad \sum_b p_b=1,
$$
with default $p_b=\tfrac13$ or inflow-weighted. (Eq. (16))

At this moment, there need not be “two sinks”. The expansion is purely **mechanical** and preserves budget (Section 7).

### E.3 Division mechanism: symmetry breaking into two attractors

After expansion, run the standard reflexive loop (Sections 2–7). Two generic routes yield **two sinks**:

#### Route 1: spontaneous fission (no extra rule)

If the expanded module is in a regime where two column families develop persistently different potentials, then the flow reorganizes:

* potentials $\Phi_{s_1},\Phi_{s_2},\Phi_{s_3}$ separate,
* flux concentrates so that two satellites become **mutually reinforcing minima**,
* one satellite (often the “middle” column, depending on parameters) becomes a transient conduit rather than the final attractor.

Concretely, within some $T$ steps after expansion, you may observe:
$$
\max_{j}J_{s_A j}\le 0,\ \exists j: J_{j s_A}>0,\qquad
\max_{j}J_{s_B j}\le 0,\ \exists j: J_{j s_B}>0,
$$
for two distinct nodes $s_A\neq s_B$ inside the module, meaning both satisfy the sink condition (Eq. (9)).

The original basin $B_s$ then splits into two basins:
$$
B_s \approx B_{s_A}\ \cup\ B_{s_B},\qquad B_{s_A}\cap B_{s_B}=\varnothing,
$$
with the separator determined by the successor map $f$ (Eq. (8)).

**Interpretation:** mechanical refinement created enough degrees of freedom for the reflexive flow to realize a true **identity bifurcation** (cell division) without imposing “two sinks” by fiat.

#### Route 2 (optional): deterministic bipolarization inside the module

If you want division to be reproducible (less dependent on noise / asymmetries), you may add a single optional, local “bipolarization” step **inside Expand** that remains consistent with Option-A mechanics:

* designate two satellites as **prospective poles**, e.g. $s_1$ and $s_3$,
* initialize internal bond weights so that $c$ is weakly coupled to the poles relative to the pole-to-boundary coupling, or
* set $p_1=p_3=\tfrac12, p_2=0$ in Eq. (16) (or small $p_2$).

This does **not** create sinks directly; it only biases the initial conditions so that the subsequent reflexive flow stabilizes two sinks more reliably.

### E.4 What “two daughter cells” means in G-RC-9 terms

A “daughter cell” corresponds to a sink plus its basin at time (k+T):

* Daughter A: $(s_A, B_{s_A})$
* Daughter B: $(s_B, B_{s_B})$

Division is complete when the two basins are both stable over time (their sink identities persist and basin mass does not immediately collapse back into one).

A practical completion criterion is:
$$
|S^{(k+t)}\cap {s_A,s_B}|=2\ \text{for all}\ t\in[t_0,t_0+\Delta],
$$
for some persistence window (\Delta), plus a minimum basin mass threshold:
$$
\sum_{i\in B_{s_A}} C_i \ge m_{\min},\qquad \sum_{i\in B_{s_B}} C_i \ge m_{\min}.
$$

### E.5 Why this resolves the “two sinks” mismatch

GRC-v2 “soft split” directly created two sinks as the event. In G-RC-9:

* the event creates a **module** (new mechanical degrees of freedom),
* the reflexive dynamics then *may* (and in division-like regimes: typically does) produce **two sinks**,
* so “two identities from a spark” is an **emergent outcome**, not a hard-coded topology operator.

**Remark (soft_split vs expansion).** The GRC-v2 *soft_split* operator is a convenient implementation of topology change on a substrate that is not itself refined: it regularizes the event by enforcing an explicit two-well outcome and smoothing the transition in time. GRC-9 instead treats a spark as **local chart refinement**: Expand introduces new mechanical degrees of freedom in a deterministic, column-structured way, and then leaves identity formation to the unchanged reflexive loop. In the GRC‑v3 reading, this same event is interpreted as a refinement of a basin chart into a richer local substrate whose post-event basin attributes may stabilize one or more child identities. In this setting, “division” corresponds to the **emergence** of two stable sinks after refinement, rather than being imposed by the event rule. This yields a cleaner separation between $i$ *when refinement is required* (spark trigger) and $ii$ *what identities are realized* (emergent attractors), while preserving exact budget conservation and enabling richer post-spark outcomes (symmetric/asymmetric division, or multi-polar differentiation) without changing the event definition.

## Appendix G. G‑RC‑9 as a GRC‑v3 Specialization

This appendix states how the G‑RC‑9 family lifts from its original GRC‑v2 reading into the **GRC‑v3** framework without changing the nine‑slot mechanics. The rule is simple: keep the port‑graph substrate, keep mechanical refinement, keep the core loop, and reinterpret the state using the richer semantics of GRC‑v3.

### G.1 What remains unchanged

The following parts of G‑RC‑9 stay exactly as they are in the main text:

1. The port‑labeled graph substrate $\Gamma=(V,E,\sigma)$ and the 3×3 bundle.
2. The local row basis for geometry and the column families for deterministic interface handling.
3. The coherence functional, node potential, flux law, continuity update, and exact budget preservation.
4. Mechanical spark expansion via local module replacement.
5. Invertible column coarse‑graining $\mathcal G$ and Split.

So GRC‑v3 does **not** replace G‑RC‑9. It gives a richer reading of what the nodes, sparks, and conserved budget mean.

### G.2 Nine-slot nodes as basin charts with mechanical interface data

In the original paper, a node carries the scalar coherence $C_i$ together with its nine-slot boundary organization. In the GRC‑v3 lift, each such node is read as a **basin chart** augmented by port structure.

For each node $i$, define the GRC‑v3 basin-attribute bundle

<span style="float:right;">(G1)</span>

$$
\mathcal B_i=
\bigl(
C_i,\ \mathbf g_i,\ \widetilde H_i,\ \mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net}},\ M_i,\ \mathrm{id}_i,\ \mathrm{parent}_i,\ \mathrm{depth}_i
\bigr),
$$

where:

- $C_i$ is the node coherence already present in G‑RC‑9,
- $\mathbf g_i$ is a discrete gradient summary computed from the row-organized neighborhood,
- $\widetilde H_i$ is the signed basin Hessian in the GRC‑v3 sense,
- $\mathbf J_i^{\mathrm{net}}$ is the net flux summary,
- $M_i$ is the effective basin mass associated to the attractor represented by the node,
- and the final three entries carry explicit hierarchy.

The nine-slot structure is therefore not superseded. It becomes the **mechanical interface layer** of a node whose semantic role is now that of a basin chart.

### G.3 Row-based gradient and Hessian summaries on the 3×3 bundle

Because G‑RC‑9 already resolves the neighborhood by mode rows, the GRC‑v3 differential summaries can be defined without introducing any new primitive:

<span style="float:right;">(G2)</span>

$$
\mathbf g_i
:=
\sum_{a=1}^{3}
\left(
\frac{1}{Z_i^{(a)}}\sum_{j:\,(i,j)\in\mathcal T_i^{(a)}} w_{ij}(C_j-C_i)
\right)\mathbf e_a,
\qquad
Z_i^{(a)}:=\sum_{j:\,(i,j)\in\mathcal T_i^{(a)}} w_{ij},
$$

with the convention that the row contribution is zero when $Z_i^{(a)}=0$.

Likewise define the diagonal row-basis Hessian summary

<span style="float:right;">(G3)</span>

$$
H_i
:=
\sum_{a=1}^{3}
\left(
\frac{1}{Z_i^{(a)}}\sum_{j:\,(i,j)\in\mathcal T_i^{(a)}} w_{ij}(C_j-C_i)
\right)\mathbf e_a\otimes\mathbf e_a,
$$

and choose a global sign $s_H\in\{+1,-1\}$ so that stable basin interiors are positive-definite:

<span style="float:right;">(G4)</span>

$$
\widetilde H_i:=s_H H_i.
$$

This is the nine-slot specialization of the GRC‑v3 signed basin Hessian convention.

### G.4 Base conductance vs analytic edge labels

In the original G‑RC‑9 text, $w_{ij}$ carries both dynamical and interpretive burden. In the GRC‑v3 lift, Eq. (2) is read specifically as the **base conductance**

<span style="float:right;">(G5)</span>

$$
w_{ij}^{\mathrm{base}}:=w_{ij},
$$

used by the dynamics.

One may then store additional analytic labels without changing Eqs. (2)–(7):

<span style="float:right;">(G6)</span>

$$
\ell_{ij}^{(d)},\qquad \tau_{ij},\qquad F_{ij}:=|J_{ij}|,
$$

interpreted respectively as geometric separation, temporal delay, and functional coupling strength. On a nine-slot substrate these labels may be stored per occupied port-pair or per undirected edge; either choice is compatible with the main mechanics.

Thus G‑RC‑9 becomes a member of the GRC‑v3 family in the same sense as the general v3 paper: one conductance drives the evolution, while additional labels support interpretation and analysis.

### G.5 Tightened basin and spark semantics

Section 8 gives a valid G‑RC‑9 trigger for refinement. The GRC‑v3 lift tightens the interpretation in two steps.

First, a node is a basin seed when

<span style="float:right;">(G7)</span>

$$
\|\mathbf g_i\|<\varepsilon_\nabla
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\lambda_{\min}(\widetilde H_i)>\varepsilon_H>0.
$$

Second, a true spark is not just local flattening or valence saturation. It is a basin-interior degeneracy that leads to an attractor-count change. So use the local trigger

<span style="float:right;">(G8)</span>

$$
\deg_{\mathrm{act}}(s)=9
\quad\wedge\quad
\|\mathbf g_s\|<\varepsilon_\nabla
\quad\wedge\quad
\lambda_{\min}(\widetilde H_s)<\varepsilon_{\mathrm{spark}},
$$

possibly aided by the fast column proxy $H_s^{(b)}$ from Eq. (11), but register a completed spark only when the post-event local attractor structure gains at least one additional stable child basin.

This preserves the distinctive G‑RC‑9 mechanic:

- saturation says the chart has run out of local interface capacity,
- Hessian degeneracy says the basin geometry is locally failing,
- expansion supplies the new substrate,
- and the reflexive flow decides which child identities actually persist.

### G.6 Mechanical expansion as GRC‑v3 basin refinement

Under the GRC‑v3 reading, Expand is not merely a graph rewrite. It is a **basin refinement operator**.

The parent node $s$ with basin attributes $\mathcal B_s$ is replaced by a small module that:

- preserves the parent's coherence budget,
- preserves the parent's external interface families by column,
- increases local representational capacity,
- and creates room for one or more child basin charts to emerge.

The new module can therefore be read as a local chart atlas for the former parent basin. If two or more stable sinks later emerge inside the module, those are not arbitrary new graph nodes; they are the child identities of the parent basin in the explicit GRC‑v3 hierarchy.

### G.7 Discrete choice, collapse, and learning on the nine-slot substrate

The GRC‑v3 appendices define discrete choice and collapse using sink-compatibility scores. The same construction applies here with one extra advantage: the 3×3 structure provides a canonical local decomposition of the competing continuation channels.

In particular:

- a **choice regime** occurs when the current row/column-organized geometry supports more than one viable sink-directed continuation,
- a **collapse** occurs when flux routing and basin assignments concentrate onto one child or sibling basin,
- and **learning** is the persistent post-collapse deformation of $(C_i,\mathbf g_i,\widetilde H_i,K_i,w_{ij})$ together with the module's hierarchy labels.

Thus the G‑RC‑9 family does not merely inherit GRC‑v3 choice/collapse semantics abstractly. It provides a concrete mechanical substrate on which those semantics can be inspected at the level of ports, rows, columns, and refined modules.

### G.8 Conservation law as quadrature, not bookkeeping

Section 7 already enforces exact budget preservation. In the GRC‑v3 lift, this sum is interpreted as the discrete quadrature analogue of the RC invariant.

Let $\mu_i>0$ be the local cell measure associated with node $i$. Then the conserved node mass is

<span style="float:right;">(G9)</span>

$$
q_i:=\mu_i C_i,
\qquad
B=\sum_i q_i.
$$

The main text uses the unit-measure convention $\mu_i\equiv 1$, so the budget there is

<span style="float:right;">(G10)</span>

$$
B=\sum_i C_i.
$$

This should be read as the unit-cell quadrature version of $\int_{\Omega_t} C\,dV_g$, not as a different invariant. Mechanical expansion, growth, merge-back, and pruning preserve the same quantity by explicit redistribution across graph representations.

The GRC‑v3 lift of G‑RC‑9 can therefore be summarized in one sentence:

This appendix states the intended division of labor between the **G‑RC‑9** class and the more general **GRC‑v3** class.

2. The three **polarity columns** provide a stable boundary/interface partition for deterministic refinement and multiscale extraction.
3. The resulting $3\times 3$ structure is the smallest square bundle that simultaneously gives:
   a directional basis for the tensorial part of the dynamics and a deterministic interface scheme for sparks.

In the full RC or GRC‑v3 reading, a spark is best understood as a basin-interior Hessian degeneracy together with attractor-count change. G‑RC‑9 does not deny that interpretation. It simply chooses a **simpler operational trigger** in the core model:

- local saturation of representational capacity,
- together with a local instability or column-curvature proxy,

This is deliberate. The purpose of the G‑RC‑9 core is to make sparks **local, deterministic, and mechanically executable** on a finite port substrate.

If one wants:

- explicit basin attributes,
- tightened Hessian-based spark semantics,
- discrete choice/collapse formalisms,

then one should read G‑RC‑9 through the GRC‑v3 lift of Appendix G, or move directly to the broader GRC‑v3 class.

### H.4 Therefore: the simplification is a feature, not a defect

The simplifications in G‑RC‑9 do **not** diminish the point of the 9-port structure. They are what make the class distinctive.

The 9-port class is interesting because it answers a different question from generic GRC‑v3:

> What is the smallest deterministic, mechanically refinable discrete substrate on which RC-style identity, spark, and abundance can already be realized?

GRC‑v3 answers the richer semantic question. G‑RC‑9 answers the mechanical substrate question.

Both are useful, but they should not be forced to do the same job.

## Appendix I. Continuum Correspondence and Known Discretization Gaps

G‑RC‑9 is intended as a mechanically explicit **discrete specialization** of the RC family, not as a claim of literal equivalence to the continuum field theory. Relative to the 2025‑11 RC paper and the 2026‑01 geometry paper, the nine-slot construction should therefore be read as a mixture of faithful structural translation, deliberate constitutive choice, and explicit scope restriction.

### I.1 Boundary geometry versus discrete boundary management

In the continuum geometry paper, the support boundary is controlled through the induced metric itself: as coherence approaches zero near the support edge, the geometry becomes difficult to traverse, and the working-domain regularization preserves that interpretation without requiring immediate deletion of points.

G‑RC‑9 does not build that boundary-horizon mechanism directly into its core construction. Its main text enforces exact budget preservation and allows topology to be managed by discrete graph operations such as growth, refinement, merge-back, or pruning in the broader family reading. This is a legitimate discrete management rule, but it is not the same as the continuum claim that near-boundary regions become geometrically costly before support is lost. A tighter analogue would require an explicit boundary-barrier or ghost-support construction.

### I.2 Fixed nine-slot chart versus intrinsic geometry

The continuum RC theory treats spatial structure as derived from coherence and its induced geometry, with coordinates serving only as bookkeeping. G‑RC‑9 instead adopts a **fixed local 3×3 port chart**. The paper is explicit that this bundle is a constitutive convention: rows provide a minimal local directional basis, and columns provide a stable interface partition for deterministic refinement and multiscale extraction.

This makes G‑RC‑9 a discrete charted approximation to intrinsic geometry rather than a derivation of geometry from connectivity alone. The choice is deliberate and useful, but it should not be misread as proving that the continuum field itself comes with primitive nine-direction structure.

### I.3 Mechanical spark trigger versus pure Hessian degeneracy

In the continuum RC basis, a spark is fundamentally a geometric event: the Hessian loses rank and a new attractor structure becomes possible. In the core G‑RC‑9 model, by contrast, a spark is operationally tied to **finite representational capacity**. The trigger requires local valence saturation together with a local instability or column-curvature proxy.

This is one of the clearest places where the nine-slot core chooses a simpler, more mechanical rule than the full RC reading. Appendix G shows how the same family can be lifted into the richer GRC‑v3 interpretation, where basin-interior Hessian degeneracy and attractor-count change recover the tighter RC semantics. But in the core G‑RC‑9 paper, refinement is intentionally gated by substrate capacity as well as geometry.

### I.4 Spatial mechanical dynamics versus a full Lorentzian layer

The 2026‑01 geometry paper adds an optional Lorentzian spacetime layer with lapse, shift, causal cones, and proper time. G‑RC‑9 does not construct that layer in its core equations. It is a theory of spatial graph slices with a discrete update loop and a mechanical refinement rule.

In the Appendix G family lift, one may additionally store analytic labels such as geometric length and temporal delay, but those labels are not yet a full discrete Lorentzian structure. So the absence of causal cones or proper-time dynamics is a scope boundary of G‑RC‑9, not a contradiction in its stated aims.

### I.5 Discrete multiscale ladder versus a scale-indexed field

G‑RC‑9 contains an explicit multiscale mechanism through invertible column coarse-graining $\mathcal G$ and Split. This provides a deterministic local scale ladder and a clean way to inspect hierarchy across refinements.

That mechanism is not the same as the Fractal Reflexive Coherence extension, where coherence is itself indexed by a continuous scale variable $\sigma$ and multiple scales coexist simultaneously in the field description. G‑RC‑9 therefore models a discrete multiscale interface, not a full scale-resolved coherence field.

### I.6 Row-resolved anisotropy versus full tensor freedom

The continuum RC tensor can express arbitrary anisotropy at each point, subject only to the constitutive choices used to build the metric. G‑RC‑9 preserves anisotropy, but only through a compressed row-based basis: directional mismatch is accumulated over the three mode rows of the 3×3 bundle.

This is enough to produce meaningful directional stiffness and anisotropic transport in the nine-slot substrate, but it remains a reduced directional resolution compared with the full continuum tensor field. Fine-grained tensorial variation is therefore approximated through the three-row chart rather than represented with unrestricted local freedom.

### I.7 Global simulation access versus local unpredictability phenomenology

The 2025‑11 RC paper emphasizes that collapse outcomes may be globally determined while remaining locally unpredictable to observers confined to partial information. A standard simulation of G‑RC‑9, however, typically has access to the entire graph state during each update.

Accordingly, G‑RC‑9 preserves the structural basis for choice, collapse, and learning more faithfully than it preserves the phenomenology of local ignorance. That latter effect requires observer restrictions, information horizons, or restricted inspection policies at the runtime layer. It should not be attributed automatically to the bare equations of the nine-slot core.

### I.8 How these gaps should be read

These limitations are not all of one kind.

- Some are **deliberate constitutive choices** of the nine-slot substrate, especially the fixed 3×3 chart and the mechanical spark trigger.
- Some are **reduced-resolution approximations** to the continuum RC geometry, especially row-based anisotropy and discrete boundary management.
- Some are **scope boundaries** of the present paper, especially the Lorentzian layer, the full FRC scale coordinate, and observer-local unpredictability.

The precise claim of G‑RC‑9 is therefore narrower and clearer than literal continuum equivalence: it is a deterministic, mechanically refinable discrete substrate that preserves the RC loop, budget conservation, identity-basin logic, anisotropic response in a minimal local frame, and spark-driven topology refinement, while leaving several continuum refinements to richer future readings or family lifts.
